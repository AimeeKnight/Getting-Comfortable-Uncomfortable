All jobs are not created equal!

Next, all jobs are not created equal!
You may need to strike a balance between your dream job, and ok for now, because ‘a job’ is better than sitting at home doing tutorials forever.
Sure perks & high salary are nice, but if you want to grow & sustain your career, mentorship, testing, coding standards, and code reviews should all be at the top of your list.

Hard work with practical approaches

Bootcamps are a great way to jump start you're learning, but they can't take you 100% of the way.
Some things you can only learn on the job.
The industry is filled with people who've been writing code, and studying CS since they were kids.
It's also filled with people who enjoy it as a hobby
If overtime doesn’t sounds like a sacrifice you’re willing to make, might not be for you.
So, what does hard work look like?
Ex: 4 hours to Rails Bridge 4 to Rails Girls just to learn Rails in person. Ex: Driving 3 days to another bootcamp, => turn around and come home because you want a bigger challenge.
I may or may not have done those things. (With flat tires & questionable airbnb accommodations along the way.)
All that to say, if you’re super new, make sure this is something you really want.
Ton of fun, but time consuming & stressful.
You’re going to be doing less of this...
And more of this...

Hard work with practical approaches

Getting up to speed...

1st: Start with test - Read them AND write them
Reading them is best way to get up to speed on a large code base.
Writing your own makes you look professional and like you care.
2nd strategy: break stuff
Figure out what went wrong & how to fix it. Makes thing stick.

Hard work with practical approaches

The Boy Scout Rule

Work to always leave the code better than you found it.
There are tons of things that go on day to day outside of just writing code.
Regardless, you should always focus on writing maintainable code.
That means the code you write is readable, extensible, & testable.

Moving past tutorials

In order to grow, you also need to start looking past tutorials.
Instead, check out source code for the libraries you use on GitHub.
Read RFC's as well.
Easier to understand than you think, and usually the most accurate documentation.

Working Smart

Next up, let's talk about working smart.
Everyone knows the saying, ‘Failing to plan is planning to fail’.
Spend time thinking about what you’re going to implement before writing code. Ex: if your creating an API, start with Docs.
Have an opinion... be able to explain why you made the choices you made! Nothing is more painful than seeing code that's been C&P'd and you know it because it's completely off.
Understand every single line of code you write. If it’s there, it needs to have a purpose, and you should be positive it’s doing what you think it’s doing.
Refrain from blindly copying and pasting!

Practice makes permanent

Reflect then repeat.
Don't just strive to be a great developer, strive to be a great problem solver.
Languages, libraries, and frameworks are just tools. They come and go. What's popular today probably won’t be popular a decade from now.
You're going to need to be a lifelong learner.

One thing at a time

Pick one thing and stick with it - ES6, Angular. I knew I wanted to do full stack JS, but also knew I wasn't going to learn half a dozen API's and a Web UI all at once.

Mental Awareness

Take care of yourself mentally and physically. I’ve been determined to stay in shape, & stay balanced.
Every day, wake up at 6, go to the gym, and for a run before work. I do sneak in podcasts sometimes. (Sshhh!) Only when I’m feeling up for it.
I’ve learned to go easy on the caffeine. Meditation has been valuable in order to stay grounded and focused.

Stop calling yourself dumb!

It’s a self fulfilling prophecy. Self talk is definitely the time to ‘fake it till you make it’!
As much as you want your job to only be about writing code, that’s not reality.
It can be an emotional career. Whether we (yes even guys) want to admit it or not, there are going to be highs and lows.
Also be aware of The Dunning-Kruger effect.
Says there's a tendency for under performers to overestimate their abilities
and for high performers to under estimate their abilities.

When you don’t know how to do something, or you come across a tough bug remind yourself

Every time something goes wrong in your program, you have an opportunity to learn something new!

A positive outlook is huge.
Without it you can enter a pretty vicious cycle.
You work your butt off because you feel inadequate.
Then you start to feel burned out.
And that happens over and over.
Establish boundaries to avoid burnout.

Preparing yourself and the team

Imagine... you're adopting a dog

You can either go to the shelter and find an old one, or... you can get a puppy!
Sure, older dogs are usually already house trained, but they might also have some not so nice habits.
Puppies are notorious for chewing stuff up and making a mess, but given a nice environment with good training the investment can be really rewarding.
If getting a puppy sound fun, make sure you’re committed to the process, or they’ll just end up in the shelter!
In other words, realize, you're also directly responsible for the success of a junior developer.

Preparing yourself and the team

Establishing a culture of communication

Everyone should be sharing knowledge no matter what their job title is.
It builds trust and an environment where disagreements are healthy and ultimately highly constructive.
Keeping a positive attitude can empower people to work even harder than they might otherwise.
Ask us questions... some newbies find it intimidating to speak up. Check in often. You may need to watch for non-verbal queues. Ask directly, “Does this make sense”?
Learn to empathize and sincerely listen. This goes back the soft skills mentioned earlier. Whether we like it or not, there’s more to software than code.

Preparing yourself and the team

Frame conversations accordingly

You might need to explain things differently to a Jr than you would a senior developer.
A lot of the time we don’t have the same context to rely on as you do.
If you and I were watching the Olympics together and I told you 'a skater cheated their double axel and had a bad wrap’, you’d have no idea what I meant.
What I could say to you though is; 'they rotated their foot a quarter turn before taking off (cheating),
and their left leg came up above their knee in the air (wrapping) which is poor style’.
I might even get up and draw what that looks like… hint hint!
With the inverse approach:
You might be confused on what a double axel is, so you first Google that,
then you follow up by trying to figure out what the heck ‘cheated’ and ‘wrap’ mean.. in that context, and before you know it, what could be explained smoothly, has turned into a 30 minute rabbit hole.
You might think this is a pain, but it will help you grow as you’ll have to think about a problem in a very different way.

Preparing yourself and the team

Good project management

Jr's are still getting used to being able to gauge timing on projects.
Ambiguity can be tough to deal with. So, you need to set explicit expectations.
Managers and product managers should work together to ensure projects flow as smoothly.
You may want to provide additional details or constraints and break tasks down. Doing so will ultimately help a new developer progress faster.
On the flip side, it's easy to resort to having a Jr's sit idle. It all goes back to making an informed, strategic decision when hiring.

Coding Standards, Styles Guides & Linting

Comments

JSCS

JSHint Maxcomplexity

Saying goes, those who don’t think they need comments, are usually the worst offenders!
Developing a team style guide is great junior project and will make things go much smoothly during a code review.
JSCS makes this easier and can even also with documentation.
Without a doubt teams should be linting as well. Beyond the basics, there are things like Cyclomatic complexity: Number of different paths within a method or function. 5-10 = danger => refactor
This stuff is valuable to any code base.
With a junior, it means we learn best practices, and can help ourselves more. Avoiding unnecessary mistakes, and disruptions. (As with testing, this will also be at the top of a good Jr's list when evaluating companies they're interested in.

Practical approaches to collaborating productively

Code Reviews

Don’t just tell us what to do. Ask us are thoughts on doing something in the way you propose.
It forces us to think, and come up with our own thoughts.
After, tell us ‘why’ not just ‘what’ to do
Not only does that offer more technical value, it's effective in building trust too

Practical approaches to collaborating productively

Pairing

Tell us when you’re not sure. Seriously... please do this!
Don’t get too clever.
If it was hard to write, it’ll be even harder to read!
Debug pair programming can be immensely valuable
Whether people admit or not, everyone writes bugs.
More often than not, we're working with an existing code base, and learning strategies to quickly pin point an issue in code you didn't write is arguably more difficult than writing your own program from scratch.
With that said, next time you’re debugging grab us and lets walk through it together.
