I want to start off with some context first: Right now, for every person in tech looking for a job, there's about 3 open positions.
For the average occupation there a single open position, with 6 people competing for it!

How do we effectively funnel incoming developers to address this problem?

That's the problem boot camps are so good at addressing!
They're popping up everywhere, churning out highly motivated juniors, but let's be honest, compaines are usually looking for seniors.
In order to bridge this gap together, we'll audit what it actually looks like to go from bootcamp grad, to full time developer, and what senior developers need to do in order to support this process.
We all like to talk about numbers. I challenge you now, to talk about a solution, and acknowledge what this process will actually look like.
It's all about working your way through the stages of competence as smoothly as possible.

Hard work beats talent

Be confident and show how passionate you are. Literally every chance you get.
Look for ways to contribute on GitHub. Doesn’t matter what. Believe it or not, this will likely lead to jobs. I was asked to work on my first productions Rails app as a result of submitting a documentation PR to the Rails Bridge Curriculum.
You're degree doesn't really mean anything if you don't have the work ethic and passion to put it into practice.
Dave Thomas who wrote the "Pragmatic Programmer" even talked about this on a recent podcast.

Hard work with practical approaches

The industry is filled with people who have been writing code, and studying computer science since they were kids. If overtime doesn’t sounds like a sacrifice you’re willing to make, then this may not be the career for you.
What does hard work look like?
Ex: Driving four hours to attend a Rails Bridge workshop, then another four hours to attend Rails Girls when you're trying to learn. Ex: Driving three days to another bootcamp only to turn around and come home because you didn’t think it would be hard enough.
I may or may not have done those things.
People who are good at this enjoy it as a hobby too.
If you’re really, really new, make sure this is something you truly want to do.
It is definitely a ton of fun, but it can also be all consuming and stressful.
You’re going to be doing less of this...
And more of this...
First strategy is to break stuff.
Then figure out what went wrong and how to fix it. This is an excellent way to learn, as it makes thing stick!

Hard work with practical approaches

Refactoring is another strategy: Being a developer isn’t just about writing code. There are a million different things that go on day to day during a your job that you'll need to balance.
Can sometime lead to a death march, and before you know it code quality suffers, and your number of bugs goes up. With that said, don’t ever check in code that isn’t a little better off than before you started. Maintainable code is code that is easily readable, extendable, and testable by yourself and the rest of the team.
Write tests - makes you look like a grown up.

Moving past tutorials

Actually look at the source code for the libraries you use on GitHub.
Read RFC's.
Easier to understand than you think, and it’s almost always the most accurate documentation.

Working Smart

Everyone knows the saying, ‘Failing to plan is planning to fail’.
Always spend time thinking about the system you’re going to implement before writing code. For example, if your creating an API, start with Docs.
Have an opinion... be able to explain why you made the choices you made! Nothing is more painful than seeing code that's been C&P'd and you know it because it's completely off.
Understand every single line of code you write. If it’s there, it needs to have a purpose, and you need to be certain it’s doing what you think it’s doing.
Refrain from blindly copying and pasting!

Practice makes permanent

Reflect then repeat.
Don't just strive to be a great developer, strive to be a great problem solver. Languages, libraries, and frameworks are just tools. They come and go. What's popular today probably won’t be popular a decade from now.
You're going to need to be a lifelong learner.

One thing at a time

Pick one thing and stick with it - ES6, Angular. I knew I wanted to be a full stack developer, but I also knew I wasn't going to be able to learn half a dozen 5 API's and a Web UI all at once.

Mental Awareness

Take care of yourself mentally and physically. I’ve been determined to stay in shape, and am trying to remain balanced. Always wake up each day, go to the gym, and for a run before work. I do sneak in podcasts during that time sometimes. (Sshhh!) Only when I’m feeling up for it though.
I’ve learned to go easy on the caffeine. Meditation is also extremely valuable in order to stay grounded and focused.

Stop calling yourself dumb!

It’s a self fulfilling prophecy. Self talk is definitely the time to ‘fake it till you make it’!
As much as you want your job to only be about writing code, that’s not reality.
It can be an emotional career. Whether we (yes even guys) want to admit it or not, there are going to be highs and lows.
Also be aware of The Dunning-Kruger effect.
Says there's a tendency of under performers to overestimate their abilities
and for high performers to under estimate their abilities.

When you don’t know how to do something, or you come across a tough bug remind yourself

Every time something goes wrong in your program, you have an opportunity to learn something new!

A positive outlook is huge.
Without it you can enter a pretty vicious cycle
You work your butt off because you feel inadequate
Then you start to feel burned out
And that happens over and over
Establish boundaries to avoid burnout.

If getting a puppy sound fun, make sure you’re committed to the process or we’ll just end up in the shelter!
If you want to hire a junior, make sure you have the time and resources to make the investment.

Establishing a culture of communication

Everyone should be sharing knowledge no matter what their job title is.
It builds trust and an environment where disagreements are healthy and ultimately highly constructive.
Keeping a positive attitude can empower people to work even harder than they might otherwise.

You might need to explain things differently to us than you would a senior developer.
A lot of the time we don’t have the same context to rely on as you do.
If you and I were watching the Olympics together and I told you 'she cheated her double axel and had a bad wrap’, you’d have no idea what I meant.
What I could say to you though is; 'she rotated her foot a quarter turn before taking off into the air (which is called cheating), and her left leg came up above her knee in the air (that’s called wrapping) which is really poor style’.
I might even get up and draw what that looks like… hint hint!
With the inverse approach:
You might be fuzzy on what a double axel is, so you go Google that, then you follow up by trying to figure out what the heck ‘cheated’ and ‘wrap’ mean in this context, and before you know it what could be explained in one sentence, has turned into a 30 minute rabbit hole.
You might think this is a pain, but it will help you grow as you’ll have to think about a problem in a very different way.

Preparing yourself and the team

Good project management

Junior Devs are still getting used to being able to gauge timing on projects.
Ambiguity can be tough to deal with. Set explicit expectations. Managers and product need should really be working together to ensure projects flow as smoothly as possible.
You may want to provide additional details or constraints and break tasks down further for a junior. Doing so will ultimately help a new developer progress faster.
Making jr's present, not sit idle

Standards, Styles & Linting

Writing Comments

JSCS

JSHint Maxcomplexity

Developing a team standard is great junior project.
JSCS makes this easier and can even help with documentation.
Cyclomatic complexity: Number of paths within a method of function. 5-10 = danger => refactor
This stuff is valuable to any code base. With a junior, it means we learn best practices, and can better help ourselves. Avoiding unnecessary mistakes, and disruptions to you. (As with testing, this should all be at the top of your job list when evaluating the company you want to work at.)
